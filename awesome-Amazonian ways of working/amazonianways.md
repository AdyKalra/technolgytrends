## amazonian ways ![Awesome](https://cdn.rawgit.com/sindresorhus/awesome/d7305f38d29fed78fa85652e3a63e154dd8e8829/media/badge.svg)

1. **Writing is thinking, reading is understanding**
Forcing myself to organize ideas in writing before presenting them has made an enormous difference. It exposes weak reasoning, sharpens priorities, and leads to clearer conversations. What feels like extra work upfront usually saves time later — and improves the quality of decisions. I realized I had to relearn how to read and **deliberately set aside time to write** — and that made a significant difference.

2. **No data -> no point. No action plan -> no discussion.**
This is something I already believed in, but at Amazon I started applying it much more rigorously. **Opinions framed with adjectives don’t move decisions forward.** Facts do. If there’s no data to support a point, then there isn’t really an argument — just a preference. This mindset raises the bar for discussions and keeps them grounded in reality. And if it isn’t followed by an action plan, the discussion itself is also useless.

3. **High standards and speed are not opposites.**
At first, demanding the highest standards while maintaining a strong bias for action can feel contradictory. In practice, they coexist thanks to concepts like disagree and commit. Debate is encouraged. Once a decision is made, alignment is expected. This avoids endless discussions and keeps teams moving without compromising quality.

4. **What Pissed Amazon Executives Off the Most**
It wasn’t missed deadlines that got execs fired up. It wasn’t even bad news. It was something quieter…and way more dangerous:
**Being unprepared, unclear, or unaware when it mattered.**

Here’s what consistently triggered the most frustration at the highest levels:

a. **Showing up without a point of view**
↳ “We’re exploring a few things…” = not ready
↳ Execs expect: “We looked at X, Y, Z. We recommend Y. Here’s why.”

b. **Hiding behind status instead of surfacing reality**
↳ If things are off track, say it early…and bring a plan
↳ Execs don’t get mad at risk. They get mad when it surprises them

c. **Recapping instead of recommending**
↳ “Here’s what we did last week…”
↳ No. They want: “Here’s what matters, here’s what’s blocked, here’s what we’re doing next.”

d. **Sending walls of text with no signal**
↳ If it takes them 3 minutes to find the point, you’ve already lost the room
↳ Clarity = respect

e. **Making them chase decisions**
↳ “What are we doing about this?”
↳ “Who owns it?”
↳ If you don’t have an answer, they’ll assume no one does
**The execs who lead fast-moving orgs don’t expect perfection…they expect signal.**
